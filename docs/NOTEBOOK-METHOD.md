# The Notebook Method

This document explains the study method at the heart of The English Codex: building a personal handwritten codex.

## Core idea

The English Codex separates three jobs:

> The screen teaches.  
> The notebook fixes it.  
> Practice transforms it.

The website explains the rule. The notebook stores the essential version in the learner's own hand. Practice turns the knowledge into real ability.

A learner who only reads the site has done only part of the work.

## Why write by hand?

Writing by hand is slower than typing, and that is useful.

It forces the learner to slow down, choose what matters, reproduce the idea, and process the structure more deeply than passive reading.

The codex also becomes a visible record of progress. It is a personal reference book that grows with the learner.

## Where to keep the codex

The method works in any format the learner will actually use:

- paper notebook;
- binder;
- tablet notebook app;
- GoodNotes or Notability;
- Notion;
- Obsidian;
- index cards.

Physical handwriting is ideal, but consistency matters more than aesthetics.

## Study loop

Every page follows the same loop:

1. **Read** the explanation.
2. **Understand** what the rule is for.
3. **Copy** the Copy by Hand block into the codex.
4. **Create** two or three original examples.
5. **Say** some examples out loud.
6. **Correct** mistakes and add them to the Error Log.
7. **Review** after 1 day, 7 days, and 30 days.

A page is not done when it is read. It is done when it has been copied, used, corrected, and reviewed.

## Spaced review

The Codex uses a simple review schedule:

- **+1 day** — re-read the notebook page.
- **+7 days** — re-read and write one new example from memory.
- **+30 days** — explain the rule from memory.

If a review feels difficult, that page returns to the beginning of the cycle.

Difficulty is information, not failure.

## Error Log

Mistakes are valuable if they are recorded.

The learner should keep an Error Log with three parts:

| Mistake | Correction | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| I have 20 years. | I am 20 years old. | English uses `to be` for age. |

A repeated mistake tells the learner exactly what to study again.

## Notebook symbols

Learners can use simple symbols to keep the codex organized:

- star = important rule;
- question mark = unclear topic;
- date = review completed;
- box = structure or pattern;
- different color = original examples.

The symbols can change, but they should stay consistent.

## What to copy

Copy the essence, not the entire website.

Copy:

- the core pattern;
- one or two model examples;
- common mistakes that matter;
- personal examples.

Do not copy:

- long explanations word for word;
- every example;
- full exercise lists;
- decorative text.

The codex is a distilled book, not a handwritten backup of the internet.

## Realistic rhythm

A sustainable session can be simple:

1. study one new page;
2. complete reviews due that day;
3. check the Error Log;
4. write or speak a few examples.

Fifteen to thirty focused minutes can be enough if the work is consistent.

The path from A0 to C2 is long, but it is walked one page at a time.
